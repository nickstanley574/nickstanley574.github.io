My last post was in August, 5 months ago. What have I been doing during the time in between? Well a lot. In August, my manager left and I became the interim DevOps Manager and at the same time I bought a house. If that wasn’t enough, in winter as a pandemic, I started teaching intro into computer science courses at DePaul University.

I come from a family of educators, my mother, father, two aunts, and two uncles varying from primary school to higher-ed. Besides learning how to make martinis and knowing what teachers say about their students, I learned teaching at any level is more of a vocation than a profession and it's a lot of work and overtime to teach well. While I have always known teaching is hard, experiencing it is something very different.

The oddest thing from being an instructor is I now have a Rate My Professor page. I never thought back when I was looking at Rate My Professor posts when I was a freshman that I would one day have my own. Seeing the page makes me realize that students are evaluating while I am evaluating them. At the same time I am reteaching myself materials I am about to teach to students 30+ students and be ready for a litany of unknown questions which kind felt like:

Initially, I thought the hardest part of teaching was going to be the lectures . How am I going to fill hours of content and keep students engaged? After the second lecture I realized that I was not just an instructor but an entertainer.

Students could easily read the book, do the practice problems, but learning something is a lot easier when it's entertaining. That's why it's easier to learn a topic by making it entertaining. Kids will remember a MythBusters or a Magic School bus episode over reading a chapter in a textbook.

After realizing that I found lecturing fun, figuring out engaging examples and in class activities to teach core course concepts. It was enjoyable thinking about examples, thinking about homework and exam questions that evaluate a student understanding of specific topics and skills.

The hardest part for me was evaluating students' work. It was really hard for me to give lower grades at first. The main reason why I was a student not too long ago and I remember thinking, if I failed a course I basically wasted $$$$ and a lot of time.

Some students just didn’t try. Some did try and when I could I would help the students who were truly trying, but sometimes Trying is not enough in college, you need to have gained a certain level of skills for a course. It was a really hard lesson for me to learn and I talked to a fellow instructor who had been teaching at DePaul for a long time.

Though our conversation I realized grading too easily is an injustice to the student who studied and understood the material and earned their grade, it's an injustice to the student who doesn’t understand the material because you are setting them up for failure in the next level classes and it's an injustice to yourself because passing students who are not ready hurts your reputation among other instructors.

At the same time a number students inspired me, I know it a cliche that every teacher as said at some point no matter the level, but it happens. You see a student start the quarter nervous and worried getting low grades. Then, they start asking questions and coming to office hours. Midway through the quarter those low grades start to be come high grades. Seeing that happens make you feel really good and makes you want to teach again.

Overall, teaching was a lot of work but also really enjoyable. It made me a better software engineer, having to teach something really forces you know it. Since DePaul is on the quarter system most intro courses for the computer science program are in the Fall and Winter quarters. The earliest I would teach again is autumn quarter of 2021. I hope that I do it again, but only enrollment, life circumstances and time will tell.
